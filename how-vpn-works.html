How Does a VPN Work?

Understanding VPN technology in plain English‚Äîfrom encryption to protocols

From Mia: I've explained VPN technology to thousands of Australians over my fifteen-year career, from tech-savvy professionals to elderly grandparents worried about online banking. This article breaks down exactly how VPNs work‚Äîthe technology, the encryption, the protocols‚Äîin language anyone can understand. Whether you're deciding if you need a VPN or choosing between providers, understanding how these systems actually function helps you make informed decisions about your digital security.

What Is a VPN and Why Do I Need It?

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) is a technology that creates a secure, encrypted connection between your device and the internet. Think of it as a private tunnel through which all your internet traffic travels, invisible to anyone trying to peek in‚Äîincluding your Internet Service Provider, hackers on public Wi-Fi, and even government surveillance programs.

When you connect to the internet normally without a VPN, your Internet Service Provider can see every website you visit, every service you use, and every piece of data you send or receive. Under Australia's mandatory data retention laws, ISPs must store this metadata for two years, creating a detailed record of your online activities. This isn't theoretical surveillance‚Äîit's happening right now, every time you browse without protection.

A VPN solves this problem by encrypting your internet traffic and routing it through secure servers. Your ISP can see you're connected to a VPN, but they cannot see what websites you visit or what data you're transmitting. The websites you visit see the VPN server's IP address, not yours, protecting your identity and location. This fundamental principle‚Äîhiding your activities from surveillance whilst maintaining your access to the open internet‚Äîis why VPNs have become essential tools for privacy-conscious Australians.

How VPN Encryption Protects Your Data

Encryption is the cornerstone of VPN security. When you activate a VPN on your iPhone, Android device, or computer, the VPN software encrypts all your internet data before it leaves your device. This encryption transforms your readable data into coded text that appears as random gibberish to anyone intercepting it. Only your device and the VPN server possess the decryption keys necessary to decode this information.

Modern VPNs use military-grade encryption standards, typically AES-256 (Advanced Encryption Standard with 256-bit keys). To put this in perspective, AES-256 encryption is so robust that even with the most powerful supercomputers available today, it would take billions of years to break through brute force attacks. This is the same encryption standard used by banks, governments, and military organizations worldwide to protect their most sensitive information.

Understanding the Encryption Process

The encryption process happens in milliseconds, seamlessly protecting your data without you noticing. Here's how it works step-by-step:

  1. Initiation: When you activate your VPN, your device and the VPN server perform a "handshake"‚Äîexchanging encryption keys and authentication credentials securely.
  2. Encryption: Every piece of data you send (web requests, emails, messages, file uploads) is immediately encrypted by your VPN software using these keys.
  3. Transmission: The encrypted data travels through your ISP's network to the VPN server. Your ISP sees only encrypted data packets‚Äîthey cannot determine their contents.
  4. Decryption: The VPN server receives your encrypted data, decrypts it using the shared keys, and forwards your request to its destination (websites, services, apps).
  5. Return Journey: Responses from websites travel back to the VPN server, get encrypted, return to your device through the encrypted tunnel, and are decrypted by your VPN software for you to see.

This entire process‚Äîencryption, transmission, decryption, and response‚Äîhappens so quickly that you typically won't notice any delay. Quality VPN services complete this cycle in fractions of a second, which is why you can stream video, play online games, and browse the web normally whilst connected to a VPN.

Every time you use public Wi-Fi at a caf√©, hotel, or airport, you're potentially exposing your data to anyone on that network. Without encryption, a hacker with basic tools can intercept your passwords, banking information, and personal messages. VPN encryption makes this impossible‚Äîeven if someone intercepts your data, they cannot read it. This protection alone justifies the modest cost of a quality VPN subscription.

WireGuard: The Modern Standard

Recommendation Level: Highly Recommended

WireGuard is the newest VPN protocol and represents a significant leap forward in VPN technology. Released in 2020 and now widely adopted by major VPN providers in 2026, WireGuard delivers exceptional speed whilst maintaining top-tier security. Its lean codebase (about 4,000 lines compared to OpenVPN's 400,000+ lines) makes it easier to audit for security vulnerabilities and more efficient to run on devices.

Why I recommend it for Australians: WireGuard's efficiency makes it perfect for mobile devices, where battery life matters. It excels at handling network changes‚Äîwhen your phone switches between Wi-Fi and mobile data, WireGuard seamlessly maintains your VPN connection without dropping. For streaming Netflix, Stan, or Kayo Sports, WireGuard's speed advantages mean minimal buffering and high-quality video playback.

OpenVPN: The Reliable Veteran

Recommendation Level: Recommended

OpenVPN has been the gold standard of VPN protocols for over two decades. It's open-source (meaning security experts worldwide can examine and verify its code), highly secure, and extremely configurable. OpenVPN works reliably across all platforms and can be configured to bypass most VPN blocking attempts.

When to use it: If you're in a country with VPN restrictions (like China), OpenVPN's ability to disguise traffic as regular HTTPS makes it harder to block. For maximum security when handling extremely sensitive information, OpenVPN's proven track record and extensive configuration options make it the safest choice. However, it's slower than WireGuard due to its age and complexity.

IKEv2/IPSec: The Mobile Champion

Recommendation Level: Recommended for Mobile

IKEv2 (Internet Key Exchange version 2) paired with IPSec encryption offers excellent performance and stability, particularly on mobile devices. Its standout feature is automatic reconnection‚Äîif your VPN connection drops briefly (such as when driving through a tunnel or switching between networks), IKEv2 re-establishes the secure connection automatically.

Perfect for: iPhone and Android users who need reliable protection whilst moving between Wi-Fi networks and mobile data. Business travellers who work on trains, planes, and in various locations throughout the day. Anyone who values connection stability over maximum speed.

Step 1: Connection Initiation

When you open your VPN app and press "Connect," your device sends a connection request to the VPN provider's server. This request includes your account credentials (username/password or certificate) and information about which protocol and encryption methods your device supports. The VPN client software on your device handles this automatically‚Äîyou don't need to manually configure anything unless you want to.

Step 2: Authentication and Handshake

The VPN server receives your connection request and verifies your credentials against its user database. If your subscription is active and your credentials are valid, the server accepts your connection. Next comes the "handshake"‚Äîa negotiation process where your device and the server agree on which encryption algorithms, authentication methods, and protocols to use for this session. They also exchange cryptographic keys that will be used to encrypt and decrypt data.

This handshake process uses additional security measures like Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS), which generates unique encryption keys for each session. Even if somehow a hacker obtained the keys from one session, they couldn't decrypt data from previous or future sessions. Quality VPNs implement PFS as standard practice.

Step 3: Tunnel Establishment

Once authentication succeeds and encryption parameters are agreed upon, your device creates a virtual network interface‚Äîthe "tunnel." All internet traffic from your device is now routed through this encrypted tunnel instead of directly to your ISP. From your ISP's perspective, they see only encrypted data flowing between your device and the VPN server's IP address. They cannot determine which websites you're visiting or what you're doing online.

Step 4: IP Address Assignment

The VPN server assigns you a new IP address from its pool of available addresses. This IP address replaces your real IP address for all online activities during your VPN session. If you connect to a Sydney server, you get a Sydney IP address. If you connect to a US server, you get a US IP address. This is how VPNs enable you to access geo-restricted content like US Netflix or BBC iPlayer‚Äîwebsites think you're physically located where the VPN server is.

Step 5: DNS Configuration

DNS (Domain Name System) translates website names (like netflix.com) into IP addresses that computers use to locate servers. Quality VPNs route your DNS requests through their own secure DNS servers rather than your ISP's DNS. This prevents DNS leaks‚Äîa common vulnerability where your ISP could still see which websites you visit even though your data is encrypted. Testing for DNS leaks is essential to verify your VPN provides true privacy protection.

Step 6: Ongoing Communication

With the tunnel established, all your internet traffic now flows through the VPN connection. Every web request, email, message, file transfer, or streaming video is encrypted before leaving your device, travels through the secure tunnel to the VPN server, gets decrypted and forwarded to its destination, and returns along the same encrypted path. This happens continuously and automatically as long as your VPN remains connected.

What Does a VPN Hide?

Understanding what a VPN actually protects is crucial for setting realistic expectations. VPNs are powerful privacy tools, but they're not magic shields that make you completely anonymous or invulnerable. Let me clarify exactly what VPNs hide and what they don't.

What Your VPN Hides:

  • Your browsing history from your ISP: Your Internet Service Provider cannot see which websites you visit or what you do on those websites. Under Australia's data retention laws, they must log metadata, but when you use a VPN, that metadata shows only encrypted connections to VPN servers, not your actual activities.
  • Your real IP address from websites: Every website you visit sees the VPN server's IP address instead of yours. This prevents websites from determining your physical location, tracking you across different sites, or building detailed profiles of your online behaviour based on your IP address.
  • Your data from public Wi-Fi hackers: On public Wi-Fi networks, hackers often intercept unencrypted data. When you use a VPN on your phone or laptop at a caf√© or airport, your encrypted traffic is unreadable to these attackers, protecting your passwords, banking details, and personal information.
  • Your location from online services: Streaming services, websites, and apps that restrict content based on geographic location will see your VPN server's location instead of your actual location. This enables access to geo-restricted content and services.
  • Your online activities from network administrators: If you're on a work, school, or public network where administrators monitor internet usage, a VPN prevents them from seeing which websites you visit or what you do online (though they can still see you're using a VPN).

What Your VPN Doesn't Hide:

  • Your activities from the websites you visit: If you log into Facebook, Google, or any other service, those companies know exactly who you are regardless of your VPN. They track you through your login credentials, cookies, and browser fingerprinting. A VPN changes your IP address, but it doesn't prevent websites from identifying you through other means.
  • Malware and viruses: VPNs encrypt your connection but don't scan for or block malicious software. If you download a virus-infected file or visit a malicious website, your VPN won't protect you. You still need antivirus software and safe browsing practices.
  • Your activities from VPN provider: Your VPN provider can technically see your internet traffic since it passes through their servers. This is why choosing a reputable VPN with a verified no-logs policy is critical. Quality providers don't log your activities, but you're trusting them not to. Research the provider's jurisdiction, logging policies, and independent audits before subscribing.
  • Information you voluntarily provide: If you post personally identifying information on social media, fill out forms with your real details, or otherwise voluntarily disclose information, your VPN doesn't hide that. Privacy requires both technical tools and smart behaviour.

‚ö†Ô∏è Mia's Reality Check

Many VPN advertisements make exaggerated claims about providing "complete anonymity" or "total security." This is marketing hyperbole, not reality. VPNs are essential privacy tools that significantly improve your security and privacy, but they're one component of a comprehensive security strategy, not a magical solution. Combine your VPN with strong passwords, two-factor authentication, privacy-focused browsers, and smart online behaviour for genuine protection.

Does VPN Slow Down Internet Speed?

Yes, VPNs do reduce your internet speed to some degree‚Äîthis is an unavoidable consequence of encryption and routing traffic through remote servers. However, the question isn't whether VPNs slow your connection, but by how much, and whether the slowdown is noticeable in practical use.

Why VPNs Affect Speed

Several factors contribute to VPN-related speed reduction:

  • Encryption overhead: Encrypting and decrypting data requires processing power and adds milliseconds to each data packet's journey. Modern encryption algorithms are highly efficient, but they still create computational overhead.
  • Distance to VPN server: Your data must travel to the VPN server before reaching its final destination. If you're in Sydney connecting to a US server to access Netflix, your data travels to the US and back, adding latency compared to a direct connection.
  • Server load: If a VPN server is handling thousands of users simultaneously, performance can degrade. Quality VPN providers maintain enough server capacity to prevent this, but budget VPNs often oversell their infrastructure.
  • Your base internet speed: If you have a 100 Mbps connection and your VPN reduces it by 20%, you still have 80 Mbps‚Äîmore than enough for streaming 4K video. But if you have a 10 Mbps connection, a 20% reduction becomes more noticeable.

Real-World Performance: Australia 2026

Based on my testing of major VPN providers from Australian connections in 2026, here's what you can realistically expect:

  • Premium VPNs (ExpressVPN, NordVPN, Surfshark): 10-20% speed reduction with WireGuard protocol to Australian servers. Connecting to nearby servers in Sydney or Melbourne typically maintains 80-90% of your base speed.
  • International connections: Connecting to US or European servers typically reduces speeds by 30-40% due to increased distance. However, with Australia's improving internet infrastructure, this still provides sufficient speed for streaming HD video and general browsing.
  • Budget VPNs: Speed reductions of 40-60% are common, often due to overcrowded servers and inferior infrastructure. This can make streaming and video calls frustratingly slow.

Minimising Speed Impact

You can optimise VPN performance through several strategies:

  • Choose servers wisely: Connect to servers geographically close to you unless you specifically need a different location. Sydney residents should use Sydney servers, not US servers, for general browsing.
  • Use WireGuard protocol: WireGuard offers significantly better speed than OpenVPN whilst maintaining strong security. Most modern VPN apps now default to WireGuard.
  • Test different servers: Even within the same city, some servers perform better than others. Try multiple servers to find the fastest option.
  • Choose quality providers: Premium VPN services invest in better infrastructure, more servers, and higher bandwidth capacity, resulting in better speeds despite similar encryption technology.

Mia's Performance Perspective: In fifteen years of VPN testing, I've found that quality VPNs in 2026 offer excellent performance for typical Australian users. Unless you're doing activities that require absolute maximum speed (like competitive gaming with millisecond precision), the speed reduction from a premium VPN is barely noticeable. For browsing, streaming, video calls, and regular work, the privacy and security benefits far outweigh the modest performance impact.

How to Use VPN on iPhone and Android

Using a VPN on your smartphone is arguably more important than on your computer because mobile devices connect to untrusted networks constantly‚Äîcoffee shop Wi-Fi, airport networks, hotel connections, and public hotspots. Setting up a VPN on your phone is straightforward and takes just a few minutes.

VPN on iPhone Setup

Setting up a VPN on iPhone involves downloading your provider's app from the App Store and completing the initial configuration:

  1. Choose your VPN provider: Research and subscribe to a reputable VPN service. Look for providers with dedicated iOS apps, strong privacy policies, and good performance with Australian connections.
  2. Download the app: Open the App Store, search for your VPN provider's official app, and install it. Verify you're downloading the legitimate app‚Äîcheck the developer name matches your provider.
  3. Create and log in: Open the app and either create a new account or log in with your existing credentials. Most VPNs require an account even for free trials.
  4. Grant VPN permissions: iOS will ask permission to add VPN configurations to your device. This is necessary for the VPN to function‚Äîwithout this permission, the app cannot create encrypted connections.
  5. Configure settings: Most VPN apps work perfectly with default settings, but you can customise options like automatic connection, protocol selection (choose WireGuard for best performance), and kill switch activation.
  6. Connect: Tap the connect button. Your iPhone establishes an encrypted VPN connection, and you'll see a "VPN" indicator in your status bar confirming the connection is active.

How to Turn Off VPN on iPhone

Disconnecting your VPN is equally simple:

  • Method 1: Open your VPN app and tap the disconnect button.
  • Method 2: Go to Settings ‚Üí General ‚Üí VPN & Device Management ‚Üí VPN, and toggle off the VPN connection.
  • Method 3: If your VPN app supports it, use the widget or shortcut on your home screen to disconnect quickly.

Some Australians ask me why they'd want to disable VPN. Common reasons include accessing local services that block VPNs (some banking apps), troubleshooting connection issues, or conserving battery on long days when you're only using trusted networks. However, I generally recommend leaving your VPN on whenever possible for consistent protection.

VPN on Android Setup

Android VPN setup follows a similar process with slight variations:

  1. Download from Google Play: Search for your VPN provider's app in the Play Store. Verify authenticity by checking the developer name and reading reviews.
  2. Install and open: Install the app and launch it. Android may display a warning about VPN connections‚Äîthis is standard and doesn't indicate a problem.
  3. Log in: Enter your account credentials or create a new account if needed.
  4. Approve connection request: Android will ask you to confirm that you trust the app to manage VPN connections. This is a security feature‚Äîonly approve for legitimate VPN apps.
  5. Customise settings: Configure options like auto-connect on untrusted Wi-Fi, protocol selection, and split tunnelling (which lets you choose which apps use the VPN).
  6. Connect: Tap connect and verify the VPN is active by checking for the key icon in your notification bar.

How to Turn Off VPN on Android

Disconnecting your Android VPN:

  • Method 1: Open your VPN app and press disconnect.
  • Method 2: Swipe down to access Quick Settings, find the VPN notification, and tap disconnect.
  • Method 3: Go to Settings ‚Üí Network & Internet ‚Üí VPN, select your active VPN connection, and toggle it off.

üì± Mia's Mobile VPN Tips

Enable your VPN's auto-connect feature for public Wi-Fi networks. This ensures protection activates automatically whenever you join an untrusted network, eliminating the need to remember to connect manually. Also, configure your VPN to launch at startup so it's protecting you from the moment you turn on your phone. These simple settings provide continuous protection without requiring conscious effort.

Why Is My VPN Not Working?

VPN connection issues frustrate users, but most problems have straightforward solutions. After troubleshooting thousands of VPN issues for Australian clients, I've identified the most common problems and their fixes.

Cannot Connect to VPN

Possible causes and solutions:

  • Internet connection problems: VPNs require an active internet connection to establish encrypted tunnels. Verify you can browse websites without the VPN active. If your base internet isn't working, fix that first before troubleshooting VPN issues.
  • Firewall or antivirus interference: Security software sometimes blocks VPN connections. Temporarily disable your firewall or antivirus to test if they're causing the issue. If disabling them resolves the problem, add your VPN app to the allowed applications list.
  • Outdated VPN app: Check your app store for VPN updates. Providers regularly update their apps to fix bugs and improve compatibility. Running outdated versions can cause connection failures.
  • Server issues: The specific server you're trying to connect to might be down or overloaded. Try connecting to different servers in the same or different locations. Quality VPN providers maintain numerous servers precisely for this redundancy.
  • Account problems: Verify your subscription is active and hasn't expired. Log out of your VPN app and log back in to refresh your account credentials.

VPN Connects But No Internet Access

This frustrating scenario‚Äîwhere your VPN shows as connected but websites won't load‚Äîtypically indicates configuration issues:

  • DNS configuration problems: Your device might be using incorrect DNS servers. Try changing your VPN's DNS settings to use public DNS servers like Google DNS (8.8.8.8) or Cloudflare DNS (1.1.1.1).
  • Kill switch activated: If you previously lost VPN connection, your kill switch might have blocked internet access and failed to re-enable it. Disable and re-enable the kill switch, or temporarily turn it off to test.
  • IP address conflicts: Disconnect from the VPN, restart your device, and reconnect. This refreshes your IP configuration and often resolves connectivity issues.
  • Proxy or other VPN conflicts: If you have other security software, proxy settings, or multiple VPN apps installed, they might conflict. Disable any other connection-modifying software and test your VPN alone.

VPN Keeps Disconnecting

Frequent disconnections disrupt your activities and compromise security:

  • Unstable internet connection: If your base internet connection is unreliable, your VPN will disconnect frequently. Test your internet stability without VPN active. If it's dropping regularly, contact your ISP.
  • Network switching on mobile: When your phone switches between Wi-Fi and mobile data (or between different Wi-Fi networks), VPN connections can drop. Enable auto-reconnect in your VPN app settings, or switch to IKEv2 protocol which handles network changes better.
  • Power-saving features: Aggressive battery-saving modes on mobile devices sometimes kill VPN connections to conserve power. Add your VPN app to the battery optimisation exception list in your device settings.
  • Server overload: Overcrowded servers can drop connections. Switch to less popular servers or different locations. Premium VPN providers rarely have this issue due to better infrastructure investment.
  • Protocol issues: Some networks interfere with certain VPN protocols. Try switching from OpenVPN to WireGuard or IKEv2 to see if different protocols perform better on your network.

Mia's Troubleshooting Process: When clients contact me about VPN problems, I follow a systematic approach: verify the base internet works, restart both the device and router, update the VPN app, try different servers and protocols, and temporarily disable other security software. This process resolves 90% of VPN issues within minutes. If problems persist after these steps, contact your VPN provider's support team‚Äîquality providers offer 24/7 assistance and can diagnose account-specific or server-side issues you cannot resolve yourself.

Understanding What VPN Is On Phone

Many Australians notice "VPN" in their phone settings or see VPN-related notifications and wonder what this means. Let me clarify what VPN on phone actually refers to and why it matters.

When you see "VPN" in your iPhone or Android settings, it indicates that VPN configuration profiles are installed on your device. These profiles contain the technical information needed to establish VPN connections‚Äîserver addresses, authentication methods, encryption protocols, and security certificates. Installing a VPN app automatically creates these configuration profiles.

The VPN icon in your status bar (a key symbol on Android, "VPN" text on iPhone) indicates an active VPN connection. Your phone displays this notification to inform you that your internet traffic is being routed through a VPN tunnel rather than directly through your ISP. This transparency is important‚Äîyou should always know when a VPN is active or inactive.

What Is VPN Configuration?

VPN configuration refers to the settings that control how your VPN operates. These include:

  • Server addresses: The locations of VPN servers you can connect to, organised by country and city.
  • Authentication credentials: Your username and password or certificate files that verify your identity to VPN servers.
  • Protocol settings: Which VPN protocol to use (WireGuard, OpenVPN, IKEv2) and associated encryption parameters.
  • DNS servers: Which DNS servers to use for translating website names to IP addresses whilst connected.
  • Kill switch settings: Whether to block all internet access if the VPN disconnects unexpectedly.
  • Auto-connect rules: When to automatically activate the VPN (on untrusted Wi-Fi, at startup, etc.).

Quality VPN apps handle configuration automatically‚Äîyou don't need to manually configure these technical details unless you want to. However, understanding that these settings exist helps you troubleshoot issues and customise your VPN for optimal performance in different scenarios.
